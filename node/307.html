Digital India: P2P lending can play a pivotal role in financial inclusion

Updated: Apr 24, 2017, 11:01 am IST

India’s big digital push has got everyone excited, especially in the fintech sector. As big data, algorithms and intuitive technology combine, the question everyone is asking is – What will it do for India’s ambitious, yet to be achieved financial inclusion targets?

One such product in the fintech space, is Peer-to-Peer or P2P lending. The sector has clocked an impressive growth over the course of past two years and more so in the past few months. However, what is noteworthy is that P2P as a product does have the ability to make a real difference in the financial inclusion space.

Being digital – Over the course of the past few years, with businesses, services and products going digital, being online has taken over our discourse. According to “Accenture’s Digital Disruption: The Growth Multiplier” report, digital tech could power $2 trillion of global economic output by 2020. The survey states that the US is leading the race with digital revenue making up a third (33%) of output. In terms of industry sector, Financial Services is the largest contributor in terms of their digital contribution to national and global GDP. In fact the same is true for every country across the world. Financial services are leading the digital disruption and this is not without a reason.

Financial services that are simple to understand, provide good value and are now available to the hitherto neglected sections of our society are finding great favour, with people willing to adopt digital solutions.

P2P lending and borrowing ticks all the boxes in terms of being simple, rates that appeal to both borrowers and lenders and is available to individuals often neglected by banks. As a result, it is finding traction regardless of social strata, age or education.

A quick look at Faircent’s Research & Analysis Report April 2017, reveals that youngsters are quick to adapt to this digital disruption and make the most of it. 

Our lenders are young with 51% belonging to the age bracket of 30-39 years. About 36 % of the lenders are over 40 and 13 % are below the age of 30.

A similar, trend emerges with borrowers. Not only are most borrowers listed on the platform young in the age group of 25-34 years, it is clear from their current performance that they are highly responsible in their payments with low default rates than average. 

Thus, though P2P lending cuts across age groups, youngsters are finding this medium of alternative finance beneficial and lucrative. This is important because lenders and borrowers are an integral part of the system and the appeal allows P2P lending to become popular.

As digital penetration increases, we can expect services like P2P lending to gain popularity and become an essential part of financial inclusion. While there are no comparable data, Accenture says for a 10 point increase in the overall digital density of the US economy would result in $368 billion uplift to 2020 GDP. Given where India is, the social and potential impact will be much greater.

Banks and SME –It is well known in India that it is very difficult to get a loan from a bank if you are an SME. Thin files, sketchy financial reports, little or no credit history means and SME faces a herculean task in getting loans from a bank. When we talk of financial inclusion, it should not be merely limited to an individual, but also ensuring access to finance for companies of all sizes. P2P has done a great job in lending to SMEs and this is largely possible because of wide range of data sets used to arrive at a lending decision. In cases where financial details are sketchy, we arrive at an imputed income based on bank statement or even point-of-sale history. We even do a credit check of the promoters along with reference checks from the proposed borrower's suppliers, among others. A typical in this case can raise a medium term (up to three years) capex loans or a short- term (6 months to 1 year) working capital loans.

 Large parts of our population continue to be exploited by moneylenders. P2P lending with its digital play has the wherewithal to cater to this section of people. Currently, 23% of loans made active on Faircent.com are from Tier 2 and 3 cities and our data show that SMEs in tier II and III towns that are underserved by banks for business loans are in fact more disciplined in making payments and their average NPA is lower than Tier 1.

Good alternate – P2P has been popular because it has been a good alternate source for both borrowers and lenders. Nearly 26% funding requests on our platform are for consolidation of loans taken from other sources. Business funding and vehicle purchase comprises of other major loan purposes. For borrowers who have suffered at the hands of unscrupulous moneylenders, P2P provides a much cheaper, better and a transparent system of raising money.

Similarly, bank deposit rates are at an all time low and for someone with disposable income, it only makes sense to become a lender on a P2P site and receive far higher interest rate. With both borrowers and lenders having enough incentive, P2P becomes a lucrative and good alternate to try out. 

Data Source: Faircent.com Research & Analysis Report April 2017
